Calculating Pitch Rate
By Dave Jesse on June 5, 2013
0

One of the derived parameters that we deal with in FDM is the rate of pitch of the aircraft. There are conflicting requirements here, with a high pitch rate at takeoff making a clean transition from ground- to air-borne operation, and achieving the required obstacle clearance performance characteristics. On the other hand, an incorrect pitch rate at takeoff risks scraping the tail, especially on stretched versions of an aircraft. For example, the A320 is far less likely to suffer a tailscrape than it’s longer sister the A321.

During flight test the limiting attitude of the aircraft at takeoff is determined and this data is used to set the recommended operating procedure, from which Flight Data Monitoring thresholds are deduced.

The simplest way to calculate the pitch rate is to subtract one sample from the next, but with pitch attitude sampled at four times a second, it is not reasonable to criticize a pilot for something that happened for only a quarter of a second. Some turbulence may have affected the aircraft and caused a transient motion that he could not reasonably have been able to predict.

A simple calculation gives a very rough signal, and to smooth this out the calculation can be run over a longer period. This was used by FDS prior to the development of the POLARIS software, but the opportunity was taken to see if we could do even better now.

One problem with this method were that it gives poor results if the pitch attitude quantization level is poor (that is, if the pitch attitude is only measured in 0.3 deg increments, the pitch rate measured over one second was in 0.3 deg/sec increments).

A better solution is to calculate a difference over a longer period, and from experience a two-second period has been found to be a good balance between avoiding the effects of turbulence yet reflecting the pilot’s handling of short term manoeuvres such as takeoff and landing. POLARIS allows us to balance the 2-second period taking one point in the past and one point in the future.

The opportunity was taken to examine some more advanced mathematical techniques, and a “best fit” over the 2-second period was considered. (Note to geeks: We use the very fast Numpy convolve process to compute the least squares linear regression slope at each point of the data).

A picture speaks a thousand words, so here is a close-up of the process.

The blue line is the pitch attitude, and taking just the endpoints gives a green estimated slope, while the “best fit” line is in red. Over this 2-second period, the pitch rate is -1.14 deg/sec using the green calculation and -1.31 deg/sec using the red calculation. Not a great difference, but for this period the slope was generally greater than implied by measuring just the end two points.

If we do this process for each data point in turn, here is what the red and green slopes look like for an approach and landing. The blue samples are at 0.25 second increments, and the touchdown occurs near the end of the graph.

The green line can be seen to be steppy in places, especially in the middle of the chart, while the red data is a more continuous curve. The peak rates of change are similar, although the red data does compute slightly higher peak values where the motion is more transient.

A similar chart for takeoffs is presented below.

There is, to be truthful, not a great deal of difference and in general the green lines are slightly less “peaky” than the red. In practice this will reduce the number of pitch rate events very slightly and be in the pilot’s favour.

A more compelling argument for retaining the simpler solution is that the pitch rate can be computed from the table of pitch attitude values directly, giving the end user greater confidence in the processing and removing any argument that the aircraft did go from 0.5 deg to 6.5 deg in two seconds, corresponding to a pitch rate of 3.0 deg/sec.

This blog, strangely, ends with a conclusion that for all the sophistication that is available to us, sometimes the simple things work best.
